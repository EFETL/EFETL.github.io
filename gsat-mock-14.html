第35至38題為題組

Long before modern highways and airplanes connected the world, a vast network of trade routes known as the Silk Road linked East Asia with the Mediterranean. Stretching over 6,000 kilometers, the Silk Road was not a single road but a web of overland and maritime paths used by merchants, travelers, and pilgrims for more than a thousand years. The name "Silk Road" comes from the valuable silk fabric that traveled westward from China, where the technique of silk production was kept a closely guarded secret for centuries. In exchange, goods such as glassware, spices, precious stones, and horses moved eastward. However, the Silk Road carried far more than physical goods. Ideas, religions, languages, and even diseases traveled along these routes, transforming the cultures of the regions they passed through. Buddhism, for example, spread from India into Central Asia and China largely through monks who traveled along the Silk Road, carrying scriptures and establishing monasteries at oasis towns. Similarly, technologies such as papermaking and gunpowder, both invented in China, eventually reached Europe through these trade networks, sometimes taking centuries to make the journey. Life along the Silk Road was far from easy. Merchants faced harsh deserts, towering mountain passes, bandits, and unpredictable weather. Caravans of camels, often numbering in the hundreds, carried goods between oasis cities that served as crucial resting points, offering food, water, and lodging to weary travelers. The Silk Road gradually declined in importance after the 15th century, as sea routes around Africa became safer and more efficient for transporting goods between Europe and Asia. Today, the legacy of the Silk Road lives on, not only in historical artifacts and ancient cities but also in the modern "Belt and Road Initiative," a large infrastructure project that some have described as an attempt to revive the spirit of this ancient network.

Gene editing, once confined to science fiction, has become an increasingly practical tool in modern medicine and agriculture. Among the various gene-editing technologies, CRISPR has attracted the most attention due to its precision, affordability, and relative ease of use compared to earlier methods. CRISPR works like a pair of molecular scissors, allowing scientists to cut DNA at a specific location and either remove, add, or alter genetic material. This technology was originally discovered as part of a natural defense system that bacteria use to fight off viruses. Researchers later realized that the same system could be adapted to edit the genomes of plants, animals, and even humans. In agriculture, gene editing has been used to develop crops that are more resistant to disease, drought, and pests, potentially reducing the need for chemical pesticides. In medicine, scientists are exploring CRISPR's potential to treat genetic disorders by correcting the faulty genes responsible for conditions such as sickle cell anemia. Despite its promise, gene editing raises serious ethical questions. Editing the genes of human embryos, for instance, could permanently alter traits that are passed down to future generations, leading to concerns about unintended consequences and the possibility of creating genetic inequality between those who can afford such treatments and those who cannot. In 2018, a scientist's announcement that he had created the world's first gene-edited babies sparked international outrage and led to calls for stricter regulation. Many countries have since established guidelines limiting the use of gene editing in human reproduction while allowing continued research into its medical applications. As the technology continues to advance, society faces a difficult balancing act: harnessing the benefits of gene editing while carefully managing its risks and ethical implications.

Despite these concerns, the esports industry continues to expand, with some advocates pushing for its inclusion in major international sporting events. Whether or not esports will ever be recognized in the same category as traditional sports remains a topic of debate, but its cultural and economic influence is undeniable.
