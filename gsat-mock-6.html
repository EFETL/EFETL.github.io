第一部分　詞彙題（第1-10題）

1.The volunteers worked together to ______ the riverbank, removing tons of plastic waste over the weekend.

(A) contaminate
(B) cleanse
(C) neglect
(D) submerge
中譯：志工們一起合作＿＿＿河岸，在週末清除了數噸的塑膠垃圾。 正解 (B) cleanse（清潔、淨化）：cleanse the riverbank（淨化河岸）與後文「removing tons of plastic waste（清除數噸塑膠垃圾）」語意完全吻合，志工合作清理河岸正是「淨化」的行動。 (A) contaminate（污染）：污染河岸與「清除垃圾」的正向行動方向完全相反，邏輯矛盾。 (C) neglect（忽視、疏忽）：忽視河岸無法解釋「移除大量塑膠垃圾」這一具體積極的行動，語意不符。 (D) submerge（淹沒、浸沒）：submerge the riverbank（淹沒河岸）與志工「合作清理」的動作無關，且語意不通順。

2.The new policy aims to ______ small businesses by offering them tax breaks and low-interest loans.

(A) penalize
(B) overlook
(C) support
(D) restrict
中譯：這項新政策旨在透過提供稅務優惠與低利貸款來＿＿＿小型企業。 正解 (C) support（支持、扶助）：support small businesses by offering tax breaks and low-interest loans（透過稅務優惠與低利貸款扶助小型企業）語意通順，提供優惠措施正是「扶助」企業的具體做法。 (A) penalize（懲罰）：懲罰小型企業與「提供稅務優惠、低利貸款」這種給予好處的做法完全相反，邏輯矛盾。 (B) overlook（忽視）：忽視小型企業無法解釋政策中「提供稅務優惠與低利貸款」這些具體扶助措施，語意不符。 (D) restrict（限制）：限制小型企業與提供優惠、降低負擔的做法方向相反，與句意矛盾。

3.Her ______ explanation of the complicated tax law helped the audience understand it within minutes.

(A) lucid
(B) vague
(C) lengthy
(D) biased
中譯：她對複雜稅法的＿＿＿解釋，在幾分鐘內就讓聽眾理解了這個議題。 正解 (A) lucid（清晰易懂的）：a lucid explanation（清晰易懂的解釋）能讓聽眾「在幾分鐘內理解複雜的稅法」(helped the audience understand it within minutes)，正好說明了為何聽眾能迅速理解，前後因果合理。 (B) vague（模糊不清的）：模糊不清的解釋無法讓聽眾迅速理解複雜的稅法，與句意「迅速理解」的結果矛盾。 (C) lengthy（冗長的）：冗長的解釋本身不代表能讓人「在幾分鐘內」理解，且 lengthy 與「within minutes」在語感上有微妙的張力，不如 lucid 直接呼應「迅速理解」的結果。 (D) biased（有偏見的）：有偏見的解釋與「幫助聽眾理解複雜稅法」這一中立、教育性的目的無關，語意不符。

4.Many farmers in the region have switched to ______ methods of irrigation to conserve water during the drought.

(A) wasteful
(B) efficient
(C) outdated
(D) hazardous
中譯：該地區的許多農民已改用＿＿＿的灌溉方法，以在乾旱期間節約用水。 正解 (B) efficient（有效率的）：efficient methods of irrigation to conserve water（有效率的灌溉方法以節約用水）語意通順，「有效率」的灌溉方式正是達成「節約用水」目標的合理方法。 (A) wasteful（浪費的）：wasteful methods（浪費的方法）與「節約用水」(conserve water) 這個目的完全矛盾，邏輯不通。 (C) outdated（過時的）：過時的灌溉方法通常效率較低，難以達到「節約用水」的效果，與句子描述農民「改用」(switched to) 新方法以節水的正向行動方向不符。 (D) hazardous（危險的）：危險的灌溉方法與「節約用水」的目的無直接關聯，語意不符。

5.The committee's decision to cancel the festival was met with ______ from local shop owners who depended on it.

(A) gratitude
(B) indifference
(C) protest
(D) approval
中譯：委員會取消這項節慶活動的決定，遭到了依賴此活動營生的當地店家＿＿＿。 正解 (C) protest（抗議）：was met with protest from local shop owners who depended on it（遭到依賴此活動的當地店家抗議）語意通順——若店家依賴此活動營生，活動被取消必然引發他們的不滿與抗議，邏輯合理。 (A) gratitude（感激）：店家依賴此節慶活動營生，若活動被取消，他們不可能對此表示「感激」，邏輯矛盾。 (B) indifference（冷漠、不在乎）：句中強調店家「依賴」(depended on) 此活動，若活動被取消，依賴它的店家不會「冷漠以對」，與邏輯不符。 (D) approval（贊同）：依賴活動營生的店家不可能對「取消活動」表示贊同，與句意完全相反。

6.Despite repeated warnings, the company continued to ______ safety regulations, putting workers at risk.

(A) violate
(B) enforce
(C) review
(D) praise
中譯：儘管再三警告，該公司仍持續＿＿＿安全規定，使工人陷入危險之中。 正解 (A) violate（違反）：continued to violate safety regulations, putting workers at risk（持續違反安全規定，使工人陷入危險）語意通順，「違反」安全規定正是導致工人陷入危險的原因，與「儘管再三警告」(despite repeated warnings) 形成合理的因果與轉折關係。 (B) enforce（執行、強制實施）：執行安全規定應該能保護工人，與「使工人陷入危險」(putting workers at risk) 的結果完全相反，邏輯矛盾。 (C) review（審查）：審查安全規定本身是中立行為，無法直接導致「工人陷入危險」，且與「儘管再三警告」仍持續的負面行為語境不符。 (D) praise（讚揚）：讚揚安全規定與「使工人陷入危險」的結果毫無因果關聯，語意不符。

7.The museum's new exhibit ______ visitors on a journey through three thousand years of human history.

(A) discourages
(B) guides
(C) abandons
(D) restricts
中譯：這間博物館的新展覽＿＿＿參觀者走過三千年人類歷史的旅程。 正解 (B) guides（引導、帶領）：guides visitors on a journey through three thousand years of human history（引導參觀者走過三千年人類歷史的旅程）語意通順，展覽的功能正是「引導」參觀者依序體驗、了解歷史內容。 (A) discourages（使氣餒、勸阻）：discourages visitors on a journey（使參觀者對旅程感到氣餒）語意不通，且與展覽吸引參觀者體驗歷史的正向目的相反。 (C) abandons（拋棄）：abandons visitors on a journey（在旅程中拋棄參觀者）語意荒謬，與展覽應提供的引導功能完全相反。 (D) restricts（限制）：restricts visitors on a journey（在旅程中限制參觀者）與展覽「引導參觀者體驗歷史」的正向設計目的不符。

8.The athlete remained ______ throughout the long recovery process, never once complaining about the pain.

(A) stubborn
(B) cheerful
(C) anxious
(D) careless
中譯：這位運動員在漫長的復健過程中始終保持＿＿＿，從未抱怨過疼痛。 正解 (B) cheerful（開朗的、樂觀的）：remained cheerful...never once complaining about the pain（始終保持開朗、從未抱怨疼痛）語意通順，「開朗」的態度與「不抱怨」的行為相互呼應，描述出運動員積極正向的心態。 (A) stubborn（頑固的）：頑固的態度與「從未抱怨疼痛」之間沒有直接的邏輯關聯，stubborn 強調的是「堅持己見、不肯改變」，並非「保持愉快、不抱怨」的正向情緒特質。 (C) anxious（焦慮的）：焦慮的人通常容易表現出不安或抱怨，與「從未抱怨」(never once complaining) 的描述方向不符。 (D) careless（粗心的）：粗心與「面對長期復健過程中是否抱怨疼痛」毫無邏輯關聯，語意不符。

9.Scientists were ______ by the unexpected results of the experiment and decided to repeat the test.

(A) puzzled
(B) satisfied
(C) bored
(D) convinced
中譯：科學家們對實驗的意外結果感到＿＿＿，因此決定重複進行測試。 正解 (A) puzzled（感到困惑的）：were puzzled by the unexpected results...and decided to repeat the test（對意外結果感到困惑，因此決定重複測試）語意通順，正是因為「意外結果」(unexpected results) 令人困惑，才會促使科學家「重複測試」以求解答，因果關係合理。 (B) satisfied（感到滿意的）：若科學家對結果感到滿意，通常不會需要「重複進行測試」來查明原因，與後文行動的動機不符。 (C) bored（感到無聊的）：無聊的情緒與「意外結果」及「決定重複測試」之間缺乏合理的因果關聯，語意不符。 (D) convinced（被說服、確信的）：若科學家已經「確信」結果，便不需要「重複進行測試」來驗證，與句子描述的行動邏輯矛盾。

10.The city council voted to ______ funding for the public library, allowing it to extend its opening hours.

(A) withdraw
(B) increase
(C) suspend

In 1856, a group of quarry workers in Germany's Neander Valley uncovered a set of strange bones that would eventually transform the way humans understand their own origins. The bones belonged to what scientists later named Homo neanderthalensis, or Neanderthals—a species of early humans that lived in Europe and parts of Asia for hundreds of thousands of years before disappearing roughly 40,000 years ago. For much of the 20th century, Neanderthals were portrayed in popular culture as dim-witted, brutish creatures, a stereotype that has only recently been overturned by new archaeological discoveries. Excavations at sites across Europe have revealed that Neanderthals were far more sophisticated than once believed. They made tools from stone and bone, used fire for cooking and warmth, and even created simple jewelry from shells and eagle talons. Evidence from several burial sites suggests that Neanderthals may have buried their dead with care, possibly placing flowers or tools alongside the bodies—behavior that hints at symbolic thinking and perhaps even early forms of ritual. Perhaps the most surprising discovery came from genetic research. By extracting and analyzing DNA from ancient Neanderthal bones, scientists confirmed that Neanderthals interbred with early modern humans. As a result, most people living outside Africa today carry small fragments of Neanderthal DNA in their genomes, inherited from these ancient encounters tens of thousands of years ago. Some of these genetic fragments are believed to influence traits such as skin pigmentation, immune response, and even sleep patterns. The question of why Neanderthals eventually disappeared remains hotly debated among scientists. Theories range from competition with modern humans for resources, to climate change that disrupted their environment, to the possibility that they were simply absorbed into modern human populations through interbreeding. Whatever the cause, the story of the Neanderthals continues to be rewritten as new fossils are unearthed and new technologies allow scientists to extract ever more information from ancient bones.

When most people think of recycling, they imagine glass bottles, aluminum cans, or paper being collected and processed into new products. But a less familiar form of recycling is quietly becoming an important part of efforts to reduce construction waste: the reuse of old buildings and their materials, a practice sometimes called "deconstruction." Traditionally, when a building reaches the end of its useful life, it is demolished, and the resulting debris—wood, concrete, metal, and more—is sent to a landfill. This process is fast and inexpensive, but it generates an enormous amount of waste. In some countries, construction and demolition debris accounts for more than a third of all solid waste produced each year. Deconstruction takes a different approach. Instead of knocking a building down all at once, workers carefully take it apart piece by piece, salvaging materials that can be reused or resold. Wooden beams, bricks, doors, windows, and even decorative fixtures can often be removed intact and sold to companies that specialize in reclaimed building materials. These materials are popular among builders and designers who value their unique appearance and the story behind them—a beam from a century-old barn, for example, carries a history that new lumber cannot replicate. Deconstruction is more labor-intensive than demolition, which means it usually costs more and takes longer. However, supporters argue that the benefits outweigh the costs. Salvaged materials can be sold, offsetting some of the additional labor expenses. Communities benefit from reduced landfill waste, and the demand for new raw materials—such as freshly cut timber—decreases. Some cities have begun offering tax incentives or requiring deconstruction instead of demolition for certain older buildings, hoping to encourage the practice on a wider scale. As awareness of construction waste grows, deconstruction may shift from a niche practice to a standard part of how cities manage aging buildings.

For most of human history, light came only from fire—candles, oil lamps, and torches that flickered weakly and required constant attention. The invention of the electric light bulb in the late 19th century, often credited to Thomas Edison though many inventors contributed to its development, changed daily life in ways that are easy to take for granted today. Before electric lighting became widespread, the rhythm of human life was largely dictated by the sun. Most people woke at dawn and went to bed shortly after dusk, since lighting a home with candles or oil lamps was expensive and produced only dim, uneven light. Factories that wanted to operate after dark faced similar limitations, which restricted how much work could be done in a single day. The spread of electric lighting in cities during the late 1800s and early 1900s extended the day in a way that had never been possible before. Streets that had once gone dark at sunset stayed lit, allowing shops, theaters, and restaurants to stay open later. Factories could run multiple shifts around the clock, increasing production but also raising new questions about working hours and labor conditions. Cities themselves began to take on a new character, with bright avenues and illuminated buildings becoming symbols of modernity and progress. Electric lighting also had effects beyond economics. Researchers have found that exposure to artificial light at night can disrupt the body's natural sleep-wake cycle, known as the circadian rhythm. Today, with smartphones, computer screens, and city lights creating what scientists call "light pollution," many people sleep less and less soundly than their ancestors did. Some cities have begun experimenting with "dark sky" initiatives, dimming streetlights during certain hours or using lighting designs that reduce glare, in an effort to balance the benefits of artificial light with its hidden costs to human health and the natural environment.
