When people think of deserts, they often imagine endless stretches of sand with little or no life. In reality, deserts are home to an astonishing variety of plants and animals that have evolved remarkable adaptations to survive in some of the harshest conditions on Earth. The Sonoran Desert, which spans parts of the southwestern United States and Mexico, is one of the most biologically diverse deserts in the world, hosting thousands of plant and animal species. One of the most iconic plants of the Sonoran Desert is the saguaro cactus, which can grow over 12 meters tall and live for more than 150 years. Saguaros have a unique ability to store large amounts of water in their thick stems, expanding like an accordion after rainfall and slowly shrinking during dry periods. Their flowers, which bloom only at night and last for less than 24 hours, are pollinated primarily by bats and moths that are active after dark. Desert animals have developed equally impressive adaptations. The kangaroo rat, for example, can survive without ever drinking water, obtaining all the moisture it needs from the seeds it eats. Many desert animals are also nocturnal, remaining inactive during the scorching daytime heat and emerging at night when temperatures drop significantly. Some species, such as certain desert tortoises, can survive for long periods by drastically slowing their metabolism, entering a state similar to hibernation during the hottest or driest months. Despite their resilience, desert ecosystems are increasingly threatened by urban development, off-road vehicle use, and climate change. Rising temperatures and shifting rainfall patterns could push some desert species beyond their ability to adapt. Conservationists argue that protecting these seemingly empty landscapes is just as important as protecting rainforests, since deserts harbor unique species found nowhere else on Earth.

For most of the twentieth century, left-handedness was often viewed negatively, with left-handed children sometimes forced to write with their right hand in school. Today, attitudes have changed considerably, but left-handed people still represent only about 10 percent of the population, a percentage that has remained remarkably stable across cultures and throughout history. Scientists have long been puzzled by why left-handedness exists at all, given that it appears to put left-handed individuals at a disadvantage in a world designed primarily for right-handed people, from scissors and desks to musical instruments and sports equipment. If left-handedness offers no benefit, evolutionary theory would predict that it should have disappeared over time. Yet it persists at a consistent rate across generations. One theory suggests that left-handedness may provide an advantage in certain competitive situations, particularly in sports involving direct opposition, such as fencing, boxing, or tennis. Because most opponents are right-handed and unaccustomed to facing a left-handed competitor, left-handed athletes may have a strategic edge. Researchers have found that left-handed athletes are overrepresented in many one-on-one sports compared to their proportion in the general population. Other researchers have explored genetic and developmental factors, finding that handedness is influenced by a combination of genes, with no single "left-handed gene" identified. Studies of twins have shown that even identical twins, who share the same genetic code, do not always share the same dominant hand, suggesting that factors during development in the womb may also play a role. While the exact origins of left-handedness remain unclear, researchers agree that it is a normal variation in human development rather than a disorder, and that the persistence of left-handedness across human history likely reflects some form of balanced advantage, even if that advantage is not yet fully understood.

第43至46題為題組

In 1845, a famine caused by a potato disease devastated Ireland, leading to the deaths of roughly one million people and triggering mass emigration that would permanently alter the country's population and culture. At the time, a large portion of the Irish population depended heavily on potatoes as their primary food source, making the crop's failure catastrophic. The disease, caused by a fungus-like organism, spread rapidly through potato fields, turning healthy plants black and rotten within days. While the immediate cause of the famine was the crop disease, historians widely agree that the scale of the disaster was made far worse by political and economic factors. During the famine years, Ireland continued to export significant quantities of other food products, including grain and livestock, even as its own population starved. Many historians argue that government policies at the time prioritized economic principles over the immediate needs of the starving population, worsening an already dire situation. The famine triggered one of the largest waves of emigration in history, with over a million people leaving Ireland for countries such as the United States, Canada, and Australia in the years immediately following the famine. Many of these emigrants endured horrific conditions during their journeys, traveling on overcrowded ships that came to be known as "coffin ships" due to the high mortality rates aboard. The famine's legacy continues to shape Irish identity and its relationship with countries that received large numbers of Irish immigrants. Communities of Irish descent remain significant in cities across North America, and the famine is commemorated through memorials, museums, and educational programs that aim to ensure the events of this period are not forgotten.
