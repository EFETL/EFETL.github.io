第一部分　詞彙題（第1-10題）

1.The new policy aims to ______ the gap between rural and urban schools by providing extra funding to remote areas.

(A) widen
(B) narrow
(C) ignore
(D) duplicate
中譯：這項新政策旨在透過提供額外經費給偏遠地區，來＿＿＿城鄉學校之間的差距。 正解 (B) narrow（縮小）：narrow the gap（縮小差距）是常見搭配，提供額外經費給偏遠地區的目的正是要「縮小」城鄉學校之間的落差，與句意完全吻合。 (A) widen（擴大）：widen the gap（擴大差距）與「提供額外經費以改善偏遠地區」的政策目的完全相反，邏輯矛盾。 (C) ignore（忽視）：ignore the gap（忽視差距）表示對問題不予理會，但句子明確說政策「旨在」處理這個差距，並非忽視它。 (D) duplicate（複製）：duplicate the gap（複製差距）語意不通，「複製差距」並非政策可能的目標，與提供經費以改善現況的邏輯無關。

2.Volunteers worked through the night to ______ the flood victims with food, blankets, and clean water.

(A) deprive
(B) supply
(C) postpone
(D) suspect
中譯：志工們通宵工作，以食物、毛毯和乾淨的水＿＿＿洪水災民。 正解 (B) supply（供應、提供）：supply the flood victims with food, blankets, and clean water（以食物、毛毯與乾淨用水供應洪水災民）是 supply A with B 的固定句型，志工通宵工作的目的正是「提供」災民所需物資，語意通順。 (A) deprive（剝奪）：deprive A of B 表示「剝奪某人某物」，與志工提供物資給災民的善意行動完全相反，且句型搭配（with）也不符。 (C) postpone（延後）：postpone the flood victims 語意不通，postpone 通常接事件或活動（如會議、計畫），不能直接接「人」作受詞，且與「通宵工作」的積極行動不符。 (D) suspect（懷疑）：suspect the flood victims with...語意及句型皆不通，suspect 通常用於「懷疑某人涉及某事」，與志工提供物資援助的情境完全無關。

3.The professor's lecture was so ______ that several students fell asleep before it ended.

(A) tedious
(B) thrilling
(C) controversial
(D) flexible
中譯：這位教授的講課如此＿＿＿，以致於好幾位學生在講課結束前就睡著了。 正解 (A) tedious（冗長乏味的）：so...that...句型表示「如此...以致於...」，「好幾位學生在講課結束前就睡著了」這個結果，正好說明講課內容 tedious（冗長乏味），因果關係合理。 (B) thrilling（令人興奮的）：若講課是 thrilling（令人興奮的），學生應該會更專注投入，而不會睡著，與後文「學生睡著」的結果邏輯相反。 (C) controversial（具爭議性的）：controversial（具爭議性）的講課內容通常會引發討論或爭論，使聽眾更專注而非睡著，與「學生睡著」的結果不直接相關。 (D) flexible（有彈性的）：flexible 用於形容「制度、時間安排」等具有彈性，無法合理形容「講課」導致學生睡著的特質，語意不符。

4.Because the contract contained several ______ clauses, the lawyer advised the company not to sign it.

(A) genuine
(B) ambiguous
(C) reliable
(D) modest
中譯：由於合約中含有數條＿＿＿的條款，律師建議公司不要簽署。 正解 (B) ambiguous（含糊不清的）：ambiguous clauses（含糊不清的條款）會造成解讀上的爭議與風險，這正是律師建議公司「不要簽署」合約的合理原因，因果關係通順。 (A) genuine（真實的、真誠的）：genuine clauses（真實的條款）若合約條款是真實可靠的，律師不會因此建議不要簽署，與後文邏輯矛盾。 (C) reliable（可靠的）：reliable clauses（可靠的條款）同樣是正面特質，若條款可靠，沒有理由建議不要簽約，邏輯不符。 (D) modest（謙虛的、適度的）：modest 通常用於形容人的態度或數量規模適中，無法用來形容合約「條款」本身的性質，且與「建議不要簽署」的負面結果無因果關聯。

5.The hikers had to ______ their trip when a sudden storm rolled in over the mountains.

(A) abandon
(B) applaud
(C) inherit
(D) reward
中譯：當突如其來的暴風雨來襲山區時，登山客被迫＿＿＿他們的行程。 正解 (A) abandon（放棄）：abandon their trip（放棄行程）是面對「突如其來的暴風雨」(a sudden storm rolled in) 這種惡劣天候時，登山客「被迫」(had to) 採取的合理行動，因果關係通順。 (B) applaud（喝采、讚賞）：applaud their trip 語意不通，面對暴風雨來襲，登山客不可能「為自己的行程喝采」，與情境完全不符。 (C) inherit（繼承）：inherit their trip 語意不通，「繼承行程」邏輯上講不通，inherit 通常用於財產、頭銜等可被繼承的事物。 (D) reward（獎賞）：reward their trip 語意不通，reward 通常用於「獎賞某人」，而非「獎賞一個行程」，且與暴風雨來襲被迫採取行動的情境不符。

6.Her ______ remarks during the meeting offended several colleagues who had worked hard on the project.

(A) gracious
(B) tactless
(C) humble
(D) precise
中譯：她在會議中＿＿＿的言論，得罪了好幾位曾為這個專案努力工作的同事。 正解 (B) tactless（不得體的、不機智的）：tactless remarks（不得體的言論）會 offend colleagues（得罪同事），這個因果關係合理——說話不得體、不顧及他人感受的言論，正是導致同事被冒犯的原因。 (A) gracious（親切寬厚的）：gracious remarks（親切寬厚的言論）通常會讓人感到舒服愉快，不會「得罪」同事，與句意邏輯相反。 (C) humble（謙虛的）：humble remarks（謙虛的言論）一般帶有正面印象，不太可能因此「得罪」努力工作的同事，邏輯不符。 (D) precise（精確的）：precise remarks（精確的言論）本身並無冒犯性質，精確與「得罪同事」之間沒有合理的因果關係。

7.The museum's new exhibit ______ visitors with an immersive recreation of an ancient marketplace.

(A) fascinates
(B) discourages
(C) neglects
(D) penalizes
中譯：這間博物館的新展覽以一個身臨其境的古代市集重現，＿＿＿參觀者。 正解 (A) fascinates（使著迷、吸引）：an immersive recreation of an ancient marketplace（一個身臨其境的古代市集重現）這種展覽內容，很自然會「使參觀者著迷」(fascinates visitors)，語意正向且合理。 (B) discourages（使沮喪、勸阻）：discourages visitors（使參觀者沮喪/勸阻參觀者）與「身臨其境的精彩重現」這種正面描述的展覽內容方向相反，邏輯不符。 (C) neglects（忽視）：neglects visitors（忽視參觀者）語意不通，展覽「忽視」參觀者邏輯上講不通，且與「身臨其境的重現」這種積極描述不搭。 (D) penalizes（處罰）：penalizes visitors（處罰參觀者）語意完全不通，博物館展覽不會「處罰」參觀者，與整句正面描述展覽內容的語境矛盾。

8.Even after losing the first two games, the team remained ______ that they could still win the championship.

(A) doubtful
(B) optimistic
(C) indifferent
(D) ashamed
中譯：即使在輸掉前兩場比賽後，這支球隊仍然＿＿＿認為他們仍能贏得冠軍。 正解 (B) optimistic（樂觀的）：be optimistic that...（樂觀地認為...）是常見搭配，「即使輸掉前兩場比賽」(even after losing the first two games) 後仍然「樂觀地」相信能贏得冠軍，符合 even after（即使）所暗示的「逆境中仍保持信心」的轉折語氣。 (A) doubtful（懷疑的）：be doubtful that they could still win（懷疑他們仍能獲勝）與後文「they could still win the championship」這個正面、有信心的內容矛盾——若懷疑，就不會說「仍能贏得冠軍」這種肯定語氣。 (C) indifferent（不在乎的）：be indifferent that...（對...不在乎）語意上不太通順，且「不在乎是否還能贏得冠軍」與球隊通常的競爭心態不符，也無法呼應 even after 的轉折語氣。 (D) ashamed（羞愧的）：be ashamed that they could still win（為「他們仍能獲勝」這件事感到羞愧）語意矛盾，羞愧通常是因為做錯事或表現不佳，而非「仍有機會獲勝」這件正面的事。

9.The factory was fined heavily for failing to ______ with environmental regulations on waste disposal.

(A) comply
(B) compete
(C) compose
(D) compress
中譯：這家工廠因未能＿＿＿廢棄物處理的環保法規而被重罰。 正解 (A) comply（遵守、符合）：comply with environmental regulations（遵守環保法規）是固定搭配 comply with，「未能遵守環保法規」(failing to comply with environmental regulations) 正是工廠被重罰(fined heavily)的合理原因。 (B) compete（競爭）：compete with environmental regulations 語意不通，「與環保法規競爭」邏輯上講不通，compete 通常用於「與他人/機構競爭」的情境。 (C) compose（組成、創作）：compose with environmental regulations 語意及搭配皆不通，compose 通常指「創作（音樂、文章）」或「組成（成分）」，與「遵守法規」的概念無關。 (D) compress（壓縮）：compress with environmental regulations 語意不通，compress 指「壓縮（體積、檔案等）」，與「遵守法規」完全無關，且搭配介系詞 with 也不自然。

10.Due to the ______ nature of the negotiations, both sides agreed not to release any details to the press.

(A) public
(B) sensitive
(C) routine
(D) casual
中譯：由於這場談判性質＿＿＿，雙方同意不向媒體透露任何細節。 正解 (B) sensitive（敏感的）：sensitive nature of the negotiations（談判性質的敏感性）能合理解釋為何雙方「同意不向媒體透露任何細節」(agreed not to release any details to the press)——談判內容敏感，才需要保密，因果關係通順。 (A) public（公開的）：若談判性質是 public（公開的），就沒有理由「同意不向媒體透露任何細節」，邏輯完全矛盾。 (C) routine（例行的、常規的）：routine nature of the negotiations（談判性質是例行常規的）無法合理解釋「不向媒體透露細節」這種保密行為，例行性的事務通常不需要特別保密。 (D) casual（隨意的、非正式的）：casual nature of the negotiations（談判性質是隨意的）同樣無法構成「不向媒體透露細節」的合理理由，隨意、非正式的事務一般也不會涉及保密考量。

Before electricity, keepers had to climb hundreds of steps several times a night just to keep the flame 11 . It was a lonely and demanding job, often carried out in remote locations far from any town. Many keepers lived with their families in small cottages attached to the tower, growing their own vegetables and 12 on supply ships that visited only a few times a year. Sensors and computers now monitor the light and the weather, sending alerts to a central control office whenever something goes wrong. Although they are no longer essential for navigation—thanks to GPS and modern radar—many lighthouses 14 standing as protected historic landmarks. Tourists travel long distances to climb their spiral staircases and admire the views from the top. For coastal communities, these towers 15 not just a piece of maritime history but also a valuable source of tourism income.

第35至38題為題組

In 1972, a group of researchers at a Swiss pharmaceutical company stumbled upon a substance that would change organ transplantation forever. While testing soil samples collected from a remote plateau in Norway, scientists discovered a fungus that produced a compound capable of suppressing the immune system without destroying it entirely. This compound, later named cyclosporine, allowed doctors to transplant organs from one person to another with a dramatically reduced risk of rejection. Before cyclosporine became available in the early 1980s, organ transplants were extremely risky. The human immune system is designed to attack anything it identifies as foreign, including a transplanted kidney, liver, or heart. Earlier immune-suppressing drugs worked, but they often left patients dangerously vulnerable to infections, since they shut down the immune system almost completely. Cyclosporine, by contrast, targeted a specific part of the immune response, allowing patients to accept new organs while still fighting off many common illnesses. The impact was immediate and dramatic. Survival rates for kidney transplant patients rose sharply, and for the first time, heart and liver transplants became realistic options rather than experimental procedures. Transplant programs that had been struggling with poor outcomes suddenly saw success rates improve enough to justify expanding their waiting lists. Cyclosporine's discovery also changed how pharmaceutical companies searched for new drugs. Soil samples from remote corners of the globe were suddenly seen as potential treasure troves of valuable compounds, inspiring decades of bioprospecting expeditions. While newer immunosuppressant drugs have since been developed, many building on lessons learned from cyclosporine, its discovery remains a striking example of how a chance finding in nature can transform modern medicine.

Every autumn, millions of monarch butterflies leave their summer homes across the United States and Canada and begin an extraordinary journey south, traveling up to 3,000 miles to reach forests in central Mexico. What makes this migration particularly remarkable is that no single butterfly completes the round trip. The monarchs that arrive in Mexico are several generations removed from those that left the previous spring, meaning that each butterfly somehow "knows" a route it has never personally traveled. Scientists believe monarchs navigate using a combination of cues, including the position of the sun and an internal magnetic compass that allows them to sense the Earth's magnetic field. A circadian clock located in their antennae appears to help them adjust their flight direction depending on the time of day, ensuring they maintain a consistent southward course even as the sun moves across the sky. Once they reach their overwintering sites, monarchs cluster together by the millions on oyamel fir trees, forming dense colonies that help them conserve heat and survive the colder mountain temperatures. In spring, the surviving butterflies begin moving north again, laying eggs on milkweed plants along the way before dying. Their offspring continue the journey, and it typically takes three or four generations to complete the full return to Canada. Unfortunately, monarch populations have declined sharply in recent decades. Habitat loss—particularly the disappearance of milkweed due to changes in agricultural land use—combined with pesticide exposure and climate change have all contributed to the decline. Conservation groups have launched campaigns encouraging homeowners to plant milkweed gardens, hoping that even small patches of habitat can help support the species during its remarkable multigenerational journey.

When most people think of currency, they imagine coins and paper bills issued by a government. But long before national currencies existed, communities around the world relied on a surprising variety of objects to facilitate trade—including, in several Pacific island cultures, giant stone discs known as Rai stones. On the island of Yap, in Micronesia, Rai stones were carved from limestone found on islands hundreds of miles away. Some of these discs measured up to 12 feet in diameter and weighed several tons, making them far too heavy to carry from place to place during everyday transactions. Instead of physically moving the stones, the people of Yap kept track of ownership through oral history and community memory. When a stone changed hands as part of a major transaction—such as a marriage agreement or a land deal—everyone in the community simply remembered who the new owner was, even if the stone itself never moved from its original spot. In one famous account, a Rai stone was lost at sea during a storm while being transported by raft. Despite the stone resting at the bottom of the ocean, the community agreed that its value remained intact, and ownership of the sunken stone continued to be transferred in trades for generations afterward. This system may sound strange to modern observers, but some economists argue that it has more in common with contemporary digital currencies than one might expect. In both cases, the actual object—whether a giant stone or a line of computer code—rarely needs to physically move. What matters is a shared, trusted record of who owns what. Rai stones, in this sense, were an early example of a concept that underlies much of how money works today: value exists not in the object itself, but in the agreement a community makes to recognize it.
