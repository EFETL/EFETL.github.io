In 1972, a group of researchers at a Swiss pharmaceutical company stumbled upon a substance that would change organ transplantation forever. While testing soil samples collected from a remote plateau in Norway, scientists discovered a fungus that produced a compound capable of suppressing the immune system without destroying it entirely. This compound, later named cyclosporine, allowed doctors to transplant organs from one person to another with a dramatically reduced risk of rejection. Before cyclosporine became available in the early 1980s, organ transplants were extremely risky. The human immune system is designed to attack anything it identifies as foreign, including a transplanted kidney, liver, or heart. Earlier immune-suppressing drugs worked, but they often left patients dangerously vulnerable to infections, since they shut down the immune system almost completely. Cyclosporine, by contrast, targeted a specific part of the immune response, allowing patients to accept new organs while still fighting off many common illnesses. The impact was immediate and dramatic. Survival rates for kidney transplant patients rose sharply, and for the first time, heart and liver transplants became realistic options rather than experimental procedures. Transplant programs that had been struggling with poor outcomes suddenly saw success rates improve enough to justify expanding their waiting lists. Cyclosporine's discovery also changed how pharmaceutical companies searched for new drugs. Soil samples from remote corners of the globe were suddenly seen as potential treasure troves of valuable compounds, inspiring decades of bioprospecting expeditions. While newer immunosuppressant drugs have since been developed, many building on lessons learned from cyclosporine, its discovery remains a striking example of how a chance finding in nature can transform modern medicine.

Every autumn, millions of monarch butterflies leave their summer homes across the United States and Canada and begin an extraordinary journey south, traveling up to 3,000 miles to reach forests in central Mexico. What makes this migration particularly remarkable is that no single butterfly completes the round trip. The monarchs that arrive in Mexico are several generations removed from those that left the previous spring, meaning that each butterfly somehow "knows" a route it has never personally traveled. Scientists believe monarchs navigate using a combination of cues, including the position of the sun and an internal magnetic compass that allows them to sense the Earth's magnetic field. A circadian clock located in their antennae appears to help them adjust their flight direction depending on the time of day, ensuring they maintain a consistent southward course even as the sun moves across the sky. Once they reach their overwintering sites, monarchs cluster together by the millions on oyamel fir trees, forming dense colonies that help them conserve heat and survive the colder mountain temperatures. In spring, the surviving butterflies begin moving north again, laying eggs on milkweed plants along the way before dying. Their offspring continue the journey, and it typically takes three or four generations to complete the full return to Canada. Unfortunately, monarch populations have declined sharply in recent decades. Habitat loss—particularly the disappearance of milkweed due to changes in agricultural land use—combined with pesticide exposure and climate change have all contributed to the decline. Conservation groups have launched campaigns encouraging homeowners to plant milkweed gardens, hoping that even small patches of habitat can help support the species during its remarkable multigenerational journey.

When most people think of currency, they imagine coins and paper bills issued by a government. But long before national currencies existed, communities around the world relied on a surprising variety of objects to facilitate trade—including, in several Pacific island cultures, giant stone discs known as Rai stones. On the island of Yap, in Micronesia, Rai stones were carved from limestone found on islands hundreds of miles away. Some of these discs measured up to 12 feet in diameter and weighed several tons, making them far too heavy to carry from place to place during everyday transactions. Instead of physically moving the stones, the people of Yap kept track of ownership through oral history and community memory. When a stone changed hands as part of a major transaction—such as a marriage agreement or a land deal—everyone in the community simply remembered who the new owner was, even if the stone itself never moved from its original spot. In one famous account, a Rai stone was lost at sea during a storm while being transported by raft. Despite the stone resting at the bottom of the ocean, the community agreed that its value remained intact, and ownership of the sunken stone continued to be transferred in trades for generations afterward. This system may sound strange to modern observers, but some economists argue that it has more in common with contemporary digital currencies than one might expect. In both cases, the actual object—whether a giant stone or a line of computer code—rarely needs to physically move. What matters is a shared, trusted record of who owns what. Rai stones, in this sense, were an early example of a concept that underlies much of how money works today: value exists not in the object itself, but in the agreement a community makes to recognize it.
