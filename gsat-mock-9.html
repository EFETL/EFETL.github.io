第35至38題為題組

In 1846, a young Hungarian physician named Ignaz Semmelweis was assigned to a maternity ward in Vienna where an alarming number of new mothers were dying from a condition known as childbed fever. At the time, the cause of the disease was a complete mystery, and death rates in some wards reached as high as 30 percent. Semmelweis noticed something curious: mothers in a ward staffed by midwives had a much lower death rate than those in a ward staffed by doctors and medical students. After ruling out several possible explanations, he observed that the doctors often went directly from performing autopsies to examining patients without washing their hands. Semmelweis hypothesized that the doctors were unknowingly transferring some kind of invisible "cadaverous particles" from corpses to living patients. He ordered all medical staff to wash their hands with a chlorinated lime solution before examining patients. The results were immediate and dramatic: the death rate in the doctors' ward dropped from around 18 percent to under 2 percent within months. Despite this clear evidence, Semmelweis's ideas were met with hostility from much of the medical community. Many doctors were offended by the suggestion that their own hands could be spreading disease, and the germ theory of disease had not yet been established, making his findings difficult for contemporaries to explain scientifically. Semmelweis spent years trying to convince his colleagues, but his abrasive manner and lack of a clear scientific explanation worked against him. It was only decades later, after Louis Pasteur and other scientists developed germ theory, that the medical community fully recognized the importance of Semmelweis's discovery. Today, hand hygiene is considered one of the most basic and effective practices in preventing the spread of infection in hospitals worldwide.

For most of human history, maps were drawn by hand, based on the observations of travelers, sailors, and surveyors who measured distances using whatever tools were available—ropes, chains, or even their own footsteps. These early maps were often inaccurate, especially for regions far from the mapmaker's home, and errors could persist for generations before being corrected. The launch of satellites equipped with cameras and sensors completely transformed cartography. For the first time, it became possible to capture images of the entire planet's surface from above, revealing coastlines, mountain ranges, and forests with a level of detail and accuracy that ground surveys could never match. Satellite data also made it possible to update maps far more frequently, since changes such as new roads, shrinking glaciers, or shifting rivers could be detected within days rather than years. Beyond simply drawing more accurate maps, satellite imagery has become an essential tool for monitoring the environment. Scientists use satellite data to track deforestation in real time, measure the extent of polar ice, and even estimate crop yields across entire countries. During natural disasters such as floods or wildfires, satellite images help emergency responders quickly identify which areas have been affected and plan rescue efforts accordingly. Of course, satellite mapping is not without limitations. Cloud cover can block a satellite's view of the ground, and some types of imagery require enormous amounts of data storage and processing power to analyze. Nevertheless, the combination of satellite imagery with computer algorithms has made it possible to produce detailed, regularly updated maps of the entire world—something that would have seemed like pure fantasy to the mapmakers of earlier centuries.

第43至46題為題組

Octopuses are among the most intelligent invertebrates on the planet, capable of solving puzzles, opening jars, and even recognizing individual human faces. Yet what makes their intelligence particularly puzzling to scientists is the way it is organized within their bodies. Unlike humans, whose brains contain the vast majority of their neurons, octopuses distribute about two-thirds of their roughly 500 million neurons throughout their eight arms. Each arm contains enough neurons to operate with a surprising degree of independence, allowing it to taste, touch, and even make simple decisions without direct instructions from the central brain. In experiments, severed octopus arms have been observed reacting to stimuli and even attempting to grasp objects, although scientists are careful not to overstate what these isolated reactions mean about consciousness. This distributed nervous system may help explain some of the octopus's most impressive abilities. An octopus exploring a complex environment can have each arm independently investigate a different crevice or object at the same time, effectively allowing the animal to multitask in a way that would be impossible for an animal with a single centralized brain controlling every movement. Researchers studying octopus cognition face significant challenges, partly because octopuses are so different from the animals—mostly vertebrates—that scientists have traditionally used to study intelligence. Comparing an octopus brain to a human or even a mouse brain is, in some ways, like comparing two entirely different solutions to the same evolutionary problem. Octopuses and vertebrates diverged from a common ancestor hundreds of millions of years ago, long before either lineage developed anything resembling a complex brain. Studying how intelligence evolved along such a different path may offer valuable insights into what intelligence actually requires—and whether it can take forms very different from our own.
