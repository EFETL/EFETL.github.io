For centuries, maps were drawn by hand, often based on incomplete information gathered by explorers, sailors, and surveyors. Many early maps contained significant errors, blank spaces labeled "unknown," and even imaginary islands that later turned out not to exist. Today, satellite imagery and GPS technology have transformed cartography, allowing maps to be updated almost instantly and with remarkable precision. One of the most significant shifts in modern mapping is the move from static, printed maps to dynamic digital ones. Services that provide turn-by-turn navigation rely on a constant stream of data, including real-time traffic conditions, road closures, and even crowd-sourced reports from other drivers. This means that the "map" most people use today is not a fixed document but a living system that changes by the minute. Digital mapping has also opened the door to entirely new types of maps. Researchers can now create maps showing the spread of diseases, patterns of internet usage, or even the emotional tone of social media posts in different regions. These maps help scientists, governments, and businesses understand patterns that would be invisible on a traditional paper map. However, the shift to digital maps raises new questions. Because so much mapping data is collected through smartphones, areas with fewer smartphone users—often poorer or more rural regions—may be less accurately represented. Some critics worry that this could create a feedback loop, where well-mapped areas receive more attention, investment, and services, while under-mapped areas continue to be overlooked. As mapping technology continues to advance, ensuring that all regions are represented fairly remains an important challenge.

Coffee is one of the most widely consumed beverages in the world, but the journey from coffee plant to cup is far more complex than most drinkers realize. Coffee plants typically take three to five years to produce their first harvest, and even then, the cherries—the fruit that contains the coffee beans—must be picked at exactly the right moment. Cherries that are picked too early or too late can result in beans with undesirable flavors. After harvesting, the cherries must be processed to remove the outer fruit and reveal the beans inside. There are several methods of processing, each of which affects the final flavor of the coffee. In the "washed" method, the fruit is removed before the beans are dried, often resulting in a cleaner, brighter taste. In the "natural" method, the cherries are dried with the fruit still attached, allowing sugars from the fruit to be absorbed into the bean, producing a sweeter, fruitier flavor. Once dried, the beans are still green and have little aroma. It is only through roasting that coffee develops its familiar smell and taste. Roasting involves heating the beans to high temperatures for a precise amount of time, triggering chemical reactions that create hundreds of distinct flavor compounds. Even small differences in roasting time or temperature can dramatically change the final flavor, which is why skilled roasters are highly valued in the industry. Despite the popularity of coffee, the farmers who grow it often receive only a small fraction of the final retail price. This has led to the growth of certification programs that aim to ensure farmers receive fair compensation, as well as a growing market for specialty coffee, where consumers pay premium prices for beans with traceable origins and higher quality standards.

第43至46題為題組

In 1969, a group of researchers connected four computers at different universities, creating a network that would eventually become the foundation of the modern internet. At the time, few could have imagined that this experimental project, known as ARPANET, would one day connect billions of devices across the globe. The original goal was relatively modest: to allow researchers at different institutions to share computing resources and communicate more efficiently. Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, the network grew steadily, but it remained largely confined to universities, government agencies, and research institutions. A major turning point came in 1989, when British computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee proposed a new system for organizing and linking documents across computers, which he called the World Wide Web. Unlike earlier systems that required specialized knowledge to use, the Web introduced simple addresses and clickable links, making it accessible to ordinary people for the first time. The 1990s saw explosive growth as personal computers became more affordable and internet service providers began offering connections to households. Early websites were simple, often consisting of little more than text and basic images, but they laid the groundwork for the interactive, multimedia-rich internet we use today. The introduction of search engines made it possible to find information among the rapidly growing number of websites, while email transformed how people communicated across long distances. Looking back, it is remarkable how quickly the internet has reshaped nearly every aspect of daily life, from how people shop and work to how they form relationships and access information. Yet many of the technologies we now consider essential began as small experiments by researchers who were simply trying to solve practical problems, with little idea of the global transformation their work would eventually set in motion.
