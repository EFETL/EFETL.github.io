In the early 1990s, a small group of biologists proposed an unusual experiment: seal a group of people inside a sealed glass and steel structure for two years, growing their own food and recycling their own air and water, with no exchange of matter with the outside world. The project, known as Biosphere 2, was built in the Arizona desert and remains one of the most ambitious attempts to create a closed ecological system. Inside the massive structure, researchers created several miniature ecosystems, including a rainforest, a desert, a small ocean with a coral reef, and agricultural areas for growing crops. Eight "biospherians" lived inside the facility, tasked with maintaining the delicate balance of these interconnected systems while also growing enough food to feed themselves. The mission encountered numerous unexpected challenges. Oxygen levels inside the structure dropped unexpectedly, eventually requiring outside oxygen to be pumped in, which some critics argued violated the experiment's premise of complete isolation. Crop yields were also lower than expected, leaving the biospherians on a strict, low-calorie diet for much of the mission. Additionally, populations of certain insects, particularly ants, grew uncontrollably, while other species, including most pollinating insects, died off. Despite these difficulties, the biospherians completed the full two-year mission, and the project provided valuable data on the complexity of maintaining closed ecological systems. Researchers gained insights into atmospheric chemistry, the challenges of producing enough food in a limited space, and the delicate balance required to keep an ecosystem stable. While Biosphere 2 did not achieve everything its creators had hoped, it remains an important reference point for scientists studying life support systems for potential future missions to the Moon or Mars, where astronauts would similarly need to grow food and recycle resources in a closed environment.

Throughout history, certain colors have carried meanings far beyond their visual appearance, often shaping politics, religion, and social status. The color purple, for example, was once so rare and expensive to produce that it became closely associated with royalty and power. In ancient times, purple dye was extracted from a type of sea snail found along the Mediterranean coast. Producing even a small amount of dye required thousands of snails, making purple cloth extraordinarily costly. As a result, in many ancient societies, wearing purple was restricted to emperors, kings, and high-ranking officials, while ordinary citizens were forbidden from wearing the color regardless of their wealth. This association between purple and power persisted for centuries, even after the original snail-based dye was lost following the fall of certain empires. The color continued to symbolize wealth and status long after the original economic reasons for its rarity had disappeared. Everything changed in the mid-19th century, when a young chemist accidentally discovered a way to produce a vivid purple dye from coal tar while attempting to create a treatment for a disease. This discovery made purple dye cheap and widely available for the first time in history, allowing people from all social classes to wear the color. Interestingly, even though purple is no longer rare or expensive, traces of its historical association with royalty and luxury can still be found today, appearing in everything from graduation gowns to luxury product packaging. This example illustrates how cultural meanings attached to colors can persist long after the practical reasons behind them have vanished.

第43至46題為題組

In many parts of the world, traditional handicrafts that were once essential to daily life are now at risk of disappearing as younger generations pursue other careers and mass-produced alternatives become widely available. From hand-woven textiles to hand-carved furniture, these crafts often represent centuries of accumulated knowledge passed down from one generation to the next. In response, various organizations and governments have launched initiatives to document and preserve traditional craft techniques before the artisans who practice them retire or pass away. These efforts often involve recording detailed videos of the crafting process, interviewing master artisans about techniques that have never been written down, and in some cases providing financial support to encourage young people to learn these skills. Some communities have found that connecting traditional crafts to tourism can provide both economic incentives and renewed interest. Visitors who purchase handmade goods directly from artisans often pay a premium, and workshops where tourists can try crafting techniques themselves have become popular attractions in some regions. However, balancing preservation with commercial demand presents its own challenges. Critics warn that when traditional crafts become primarily oriented toward tourists, the items produced may be simplified or altered to suit foreign tastes, potentially losing the cultural significance and craftsmanship that made them valuable in the first place. Despite these tensions, many artisans and cultural advocates remain hopeful. Online marketplaces have opened up new opportunities for artisans to sell their work directly to customers around the world, sometimes allowing them to earn a sustainable living from their craft without relying entirely on local markets or tourism. Ultimately, the survival of many traditional crafts may depend on finding a balance between honoring their cultural roots and adapting to the realities of a changing economy.
